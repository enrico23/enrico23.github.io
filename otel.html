Making Observability a Platform Invariant with OpenTelemetry

Making Observability a Platform Invariant with OpenTelemetry

Case Study — Platform Observability Strategy (.NET / Microservices)

In a previous role, I worked closely with the infrastructure team during the introduction of OpenTelemetry across both a microservices landscape and a large monolithic backend.

My focus was not on instrumentation alone, but on making observability consistent, adoptable, and operationally durable at scale.

Context

  • Mixed architecture: microservices alongside a large monolith.
  • Shared observability package introduced centrally.
  • Multiple product teams consuming the platform.

The technical challenge was instrumentation. The organisational challenge was adoption and long-term consistency.

Core Lessons

  • Adoption is constrained more by cognitive load than tooling.
  • Telemetry must be visible and useful during development to be adopted.
  • Consistency matters more than configurability.

Platform-Level Decisions

1) Observability as a Default, Not an Option

Telemetry was centralised into a shared Service Defaults package.

  • Uniform logging, metrics, and tracing configuration.
  • Standard filtering and enrichment rules.
  • No per-service opt-in model.

If a service ran, it was observable. This removed variation and reduced operational drift across teams.

2) Tracing With Intent

  • Focused on user-initiated flows, outbound dependencies, and data access.
  • Filtered low-value spans early (health checks, static assets, framework noise).
  • Preserved business correlation identifiers as explicit attributes.

The goal was signal clarity, not volume.

3) Correlation That Works

  • Technical correlation via W3C trace context.
  • Business correlation IDs generated at the edge and propagated downstream.
  • Enriched both logs and traces to support cross-team workflows.

4) Guardrails Validated Early

  • Sampling strategy aligned to environment boundaries.
  • Consistent resource attributes (service name, version, environment).
  • Dependency visibility as a non-negotiable baseline.
  • Export configuration treated as a deployment concern, not a code concern.

Impact

  • Lowered adoption friction for product teams.
  • Improved cross-team debugging through predictable telemetry shape.
  • Reduced configuration drift across services.
  • Enabled consistent triage workflows for incidents.

Application in Current Work

I am applying the same principles in a .NET Aspire–based microservices demo as an independent design exercise, validating observability patterns in a controlled environment.

Why This Matters

Observability becomes strategically valuable when it is consistent, predictable, and automatic. When embedded as a platform invariant, it reduces coordination cost and preserves debuggability as systems evolve.
